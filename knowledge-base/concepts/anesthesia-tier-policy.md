# Anesthesia tier policy

Pawline's three-level classification of anesthesia procedures, gating who can administer what, with what equipment, in what clinic tier. Aligned with AAHA anesthesia guidelines but tightened for Pawline's risk envelope.

## The tiers

| Tier | Examples | Equipment required | Personnel required | Clinic tier |
|------|----------|---------------------|---------------------|-------------|
| **1 — Light** | Sedation for grooming, minor procedures, dental cleaning grade 1 | Basic monitoring (HR, SpO2) | Credentialed technician under DVM oversight | Any |
| **2 — Moderate** | Soft-tissue surgery, dental extractions, mass removals | Pulse-ox, BP, capnography, IV fluids | DVM + dedicated anesthesia tech | B and A |
| **3 — High-risk** | Orthopedics, ASA 4-5 patients, prolonged surgery, brachycephalic airway management | Full multi-parameter monitoring, ventilator, advanced airway | Surgeon DVM + dedicated DVM-supervised anesthesia | A only |

## Why the tier system

A one-size-fits-all anesthesia policy either over-equips Tier C clinics or under-protects Tier A patients. The tiering matches clinical risk to clinic capability.

## What changed in v2

The 2026-02-18 [[2026-02-18-clinical-protocol-v2-publish]] tightened Tier 3 to require an additional DVM in the room (not just a credentialed tech) for ASA 4-5 patients. This is more conservative than typical industry practice and was a deliberate clinical-safety call by [[dr-elena-vasquez]].

## Compliance audit

Tier compliance is reviewed during the [[clinical-quality-review]] quarterly chart audit. Any anesthesia incident triggers a separate root-cause analysis regardless of tier compliance.

## Tension with the M&A pipeline

Acquired clinics often arrive with looser anesthesia practices. Bringing a newly-acquired clinic into compliance is part of the [[acquisition-integration-playbook]] T+30 milestone. We don't accept "we've always done it this way" — see [[clinical-protocols-policy]].
